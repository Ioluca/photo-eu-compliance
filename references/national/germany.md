# Germany — National Photography Law Reference

## Key Legislation
- **KUG (Kunsturhebergesetz)** — Art Copyright Act, §§22-24
- **BDSG (Bundesdatenschutzgesetz)** — Federal Data Protection Act
- **State Media Laws (Landespressegesetze)** — Press freedom derogations
- **BGB §823** — General personality rights

---

## Right to Image (Recht am eigenen Bild)

### KUG §22 — General Rule
Images of a person may only be distributed or publicly displayed with
their consent. Consent is presumed if the person received payment for posing.

### KUG §23 — Exceptions (no consent needed)
1. **Persons of contemporary history** (Personen der Zeitgeschichte) —
   politicians, celebrities, public figures in public roles
2. **Images where the person is only accessory** (Beiwerk) — person is
   incidental to the main subject (e.g., pedestrian walking past a landmark)
3. **Assemblies, processions, and similar events** — crowds at public events
4. **Art** — higher artistic interest, but must respect personality rights

### KUG §24 — Dignity Override
Even when exceptions apply, publication is NOT allowed if it violates
the legitimate interests of the depicted person (particularly their dignity).

### Practical impact
Germany has the **most restrictive** right-to-image law in the EU for
photographers. Street photography of identifiable individuals is very
risky without consent. The "Beiwerk" exception is interpreted narrowly —
the person must truly be incidental, not the subject.

## GDPR Implementation

### BDSG + State Laws
Germany's GDPR implementation adds layers:
- State press laws provide journalism derogations (Art. 85 GDPR)
- Art photography falls under artistic expression, but interpretation
  varies by court
- The BDSG provides additional procedural requirements

### Key rulings
- BGH has confirmed that KUG continues to apply alongside GDPR for photography
- The "graduated protection model" (abgestuftes Schutzkonzept) applies:
  public figures get less protection in their public role, more in private life
- Event photographers must provide notice but individual consent for crowd
  shots is not required if the event itself is the subject

## AI-Specific
Germany's VG Bild-Kunst (collecting society for visual arts) has been active
in the AI copyright debate. No specific national AI photography regulation
beyond the EU AI Act.
