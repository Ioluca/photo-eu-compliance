## 1. When GDPR Applies to Photography

A photograph of a recognizable person IS personal data under GDPR.
GDPR applies when:

- You photograph identifiable individuals (face, tattoos, name tags, etc.)
- You store, organize, or process these photos digitally
- You are doing so in a professional/commercial context

GDPR does NOT apply to:
- Purely personal/household photography (family snapshots)
- Photos where no individual is identifiable
- Landscapes, objects, architecture without recognizable people

**Key case law:** The CJEU in *Ryneš* (C-212/13) confirmed that even CCTV
footage constitutes personal data processing. By extension, professional
photography of identifiable persons falls squarely under GDPR.

## 2. Legal Bases (Art. 6)

Photographers can rely on several legal bases:

### Art. 6(1)(a) — Consent
The model/subject explicitly agrees to be photographed and to the specified uses.
**Best for:** Portrait sessions, model shoots, commercial work with hired subjects.
**Implementation:** Model release form with GDPR-compliant consent language.

### Art. 6(1)(b) — Contractual necessity
Photography is necessary to fulfill a contract.
**Best for:** Wedding photography (couple hired you), corporate headshots
(company contracted the service), event coverage (event organizer contract).
**Note:** Only covers the specific contracted purpose. Reusing wedding photos
for your portfolio may need separate consent or legitimate interest.

### Art. 6(1)(f) — Legitimate interest
Your legitimate interest in taking/publishing photos outweighs the subject's
privacy rights, after a balancing test.
**Best for:** Event photography, public gatherings, press coverage, portfolio use.
**Requirements:** You must conduct a Legitimate Interest Assessment (LIA)
and document it. You must offer an opt-out mechanism.

### Art. 85 Derogation (see Section 3)
National exemptions for journalism, academic, artistic, or literary purposes.

### Choosing the right basis

| Context | Recommended basis | Notes |
|---------|------------------|-------|
| Model shoot | Consent (Art. 6(1)(a)) | Model release with GDPR section |
| Wedding | Contract (Art. 6(1)(b)) | Service contract covers the couple; guests via legitimate interest or event signage |
| Corporate event | Contract + Legitimate interest | Contract with organizer; attendee notice via signage |
| Street photography | Legitimate interest OR Art. 85 | Depends on national implementation |
| Journalism | Art. 85 derogation | National law varies significantly |
| Fine art | Art. 85 derogation | National law varies |

## 3. Journalism & Art Derogation (Art. 85)

**Art. 85(1):** Member States shall reconcile the right to data protection
with the right to freedom of expression and information, including processing
for journalistic purposes and for academic, artistic, or literary expression.

**Art. 85(2):** For processing carried out for journalistic purposes or for
academic, artistic, or literary expression, Member States shall provide
exemptions or derogations from key GDPR provisions.

**Critical point:** Art. 85 is NOT self-executing. Each Member State implements
it differently. This means:

- In France: broad exemption for journalism, narrower for art
- In Germany: press exemption via state media laws
- In Italy: Codice Privacy Art. 136-139 provides derogations for journalism
- In Spain: Organic Law 3/2018, Art. 85 provides limited derogation

Always check `references/national/[country].md` for the specific implementation.

EUR-Lex: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj#art_85

## 4. Biometric Data (Art. 9)

**Art. 9(1):** Processing of biometric data for the purpose of uniquely
identifying a natural person is prohibited unless an exception applies.

**What counts as biometric processing in photography:**
- Using facial recognition software to identify/sort people in photos
- Creating facial templates for matching purposes
- Scanning photos to build a biometric database

**What does NOT count:**
- Simply photographing a face (the photo itself is personal data, not biometric)
- Lightroom People view on a local catalog (generally personal/household use)
- Manual tagging of people by name

**Exceptions (Art. 9(2)):**
- Explicit consent (Art. 9(2)(a)) — most relevant for photographers
- Data manifestly made public by the data subject (Art. 9(2)(e)) — limited

## 5. Data Subject Rights (Art. 15-22)

When you photograph identifiable people, they have:

- **Art. 15 — Right of access:** They can ask what photos you have of them
- **Art. 16 — Right to rectification:** They can ask you to correct associated data
- **Art. 17 — Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten"):** They can request
  deletion of their photos, UNLESS you have an overriding legitimate interest
  or the photos are necessary for exercising freedom of expression (Art. 17(3)(a))
- **Art. 18 — Right to restriction:** They can ask you to stop processing
- **Art. 20 — Right to data portability:** They can request copies
- **Art. 21 — Right to object:** They can object to processing based on
  legitimate interest; you must stop unless you demonstrate compelling
  legitimate grounds

**Practical impact:** If a person you photographed at a public event
contacts you and asks you to delete their photos, you must comply
UNLESS you can demonstrate a compelling legitimate interest (e.g.,
the photo is newsworthy, or it's an artistic work already published).

## 6. Model Release under GDPR

A GDPR-compliant model release must include:

1. **Identity of the controller** (photographer's name, business, contact)
2. **Purpose of processing** (specific: "for portfolio display on website X",
   not vague: "for marketing purposes")
3. **Legal basis** (typically consent under Art. 6(1)(a))
4. **Categories of data** (photographs, name, likeness)
5. **Recipients** (who will receive the photos — clients, publishers, platforms)
6. **Retention period** (how long you keep the photos)
7. **Data subject rights** (list of rights under Art. 15-22)
8. **Right to withdraw consent** (Art. 7(3)) — must be as easy as giving consent
9. **Consequences of withdrawal** (photos must be deleted from your files,
   but note: photos already published may not be recallable)
10. **Contact for DPO** (if applicable) or privacy contact

## 7. Street Photography & Public Events

This is the most contested area. GDPR vs. artistic freedom.

**General principle:** Street photography of identifiable individuals
in public spaces requires a legal basis. "Being in public" does NOT equal
consent to be photographed.

**Practical approaches by country:**

| Country | Approach | Key law |
|---------|----------|---------|
| 🇩🇪 Germany | Very restrictive. KUG requires consent except for public figures, crowds, art | KUG §22-23 + GDPR |
| 🇫🇷 France | Restrictive. Droit à l'image requires consent; art/press exceptions exist | Code Civil Art. 9 |
| 🇮🇹 Italy | Moderate. Art. 97 LDA allows photos at public events; Art. 10 CC protects dignity | LDA Art. 96-97 + CC Art. 10 |
| 🇪🇸 Spain | Moderate. LO 1/1982 protects image rights; press/public interest exceptions | LO 1/1982 Art. 8 |

**Best practices for street photographers in all EU countries:**
- Rely on legitimate interest (Art. 6(1)(f)) with a documented balancing test
- Or rely on Art. 85 derogation if your national law supports art/journalism
- Avoid photographing vulnerable individuals (children, homeless, etc.)
- Be prepared to delete if requested (unless compelling reasons to keep)
- If publishing commercially, obtain consent wherever possible
- For event coverage: use signage informing attendees that photography is taking place

## 8. Children (Art. 8)

**Art. 8:** Consent for information society services requires parental consent
for children under 16 (or lower age set by Member State, minimum 13).

**For photographers:**
- Always obtain parental/guardian consent when photographing minors
- Include guardian signature in model releases
- Be extra cautious with school/sports photography
- Some national laws impose additional restrictions
- Portfolio/social media use of children's photos requires explicit parental consent
